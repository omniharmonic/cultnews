The members of the Basement do not believe the world is going to end. They believe it is going to be *closed* — that reality is a running simulation, that its resources are finite, and that the operators who maintain it will, before long, shut it down or let it decay.

Their theology is therefore a theology of attention. If the world is a program, they reason, then the only souls worth saving are the ones the operators are watching, and the way to be watched is to become anomalous — to do things so statistically strange that a monitor somewhere flags the log. They call these acts "raising the render." They are, by design, harmless and inexplicable: coordinated, meaningless, meticulous.

It would be easy to file the Basement under the whimsical. But its members are not playing. They speak of being "seen by the admins" with the same ache that a mystic speaks of being seen by God, and it is not clear the difference matters to them. The world offers them no evidence they are known; they have organized their lives around manufacturing the evidence.

The Data Desk classifies the Basement under Simulation, the smallest and youngest of our categories. It is also, our reporters find, the one whose members most often say the same thing when asked why now: that the world has begun to feel unreal to them, and that they would rather believe it *is* unreal than believe they simply stopped being able to feel it.
