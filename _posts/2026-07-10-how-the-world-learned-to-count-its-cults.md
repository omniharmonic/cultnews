The most-asked question on our tip line is whether the numbers are real. They are our numbers, gathered by our methods, and we are the first to say the methods are imperfect. But the shape does not depend on the decimals. In the 1970s, a busy year produced two or three new movements. This year produced nineteen before the summer, against a standing population of more than a hundred. Whatever we are measuring, there is unmistakably more of it.

The tempting explanation is that people have gotten stranger. We do not think that is it. Across every category — the accelerationists and the contactees, the ascension festivals and the frozen faithful — our reporters keep hearing one thing in different words: that the world has come to feel *thinner.* Less real, less enchanted, less able to hold a life. The movements disagree about everything except the diagnosis. Each is a different prescription for the same complaint, which is that ordinary reality stopped being enough to live inside.

Read that way, the curve is not measuring credulity. It is measuring a shortage — of meaning, of belonging, of the sense that anything is at stake — and the hundred arks are what a shortage of that kind looks like when it is graphed. Every movement we cover offers a totalizing answer: one god, one ship, one frequency, one future, one dawn. The answers are incompatible and the hunger is identical, and the hunger, our reporters increasingly suspect, is the actual story.

We keep the count because someone should. But we have started to think the count is a thermometer, not a census — that it measures less how many people have found an answer than how many have run out of the ordinary reasons to stay.
