Most simulation theories are, at bottom, engineering theories: the world is a program, run on a computer, by someone. The Lucid reject the machine. Their doctrine is older and stranger — that reality is not computed but *dreamed*, that we are figures inside the sleeping mind of a god, and that the dream has begun, subtly, to go wrong: to flatten, to loop, to lose its color, the way a long dream frays before waking.

Their practice follows from the diagnosis. If the world is a dream and the dream is decaying, then salvation is not escape but arousal — waking the dreamer. The Lucid train in techniques they compare to lucid dreaming performed from inside someone else's dream: coordinated acts of vividness, beauty, and strangeness meant to be *striking enough to stir the sleeper*, to make the god shift, surface, and open its eyes. Members describe the sense that a sufficiently intense moment — of art, of love, of terror — briefly "thins the dream" and lets the light of the waking world through.

Scholars note the Lucid have independently reinvented a very old mystical intuition — that the divine is asleep and the creation is its dream — and dressed it in the vocabulary of simulation. Readers of this publication will notice a closer rhyme: the Herd of God, too, teaches that its deity is asleep and must be woken, though the Herd wakes a bull and the Lucid wake a dreamer. Neither, asked to explain the resemblance, seemed troubled by it. The Lucid's founder said only that of course the intuition keeps recurring — a dream, near waking, tends to fill with characters trying to end it.
