They met, the way these things happen, through a mutual belief that chance is a language. On one side of the table was Duncan Horst, the Herd of God's High Priest, a former trader who now treats markets and miracles as the same subject. On the other was the man the underground still calls the Alchemist — an octogenarian director of midnight films, tarot obsessive, and founder of a healing practice built on staged coincidence — whose whole life has been an argument that art, ritual, and therapy are one act.

The game was their own invention, assembled from two decks and one shared conviction. They dealt tarot as blackjack: each card a value and an omen at once, the object to reach a number without breaking it, but every hand also read aloud as a spread. A bust was a warning; a natural was a blessing; the house was fate, and fate, they agreed, cheats. They played for hours. No money changed hands. A great deal of interpretation did.

What the two men were doing, under the game, was comparing notes on the same wager — that a disenchanted world can be re-enchanted deliberately, by people willing to treat every accident as a message. The Alchemist has spent sixty years insisting that meaning is something you *stage* until it becomes real; the Herd has built a religion on the same premise, with a bull-god at the center. Whether either is right is not a thing a card game can settle. But by the last hand, witnesses say, the two of them had stopped keeping score, which is either how you lose at blackjack or how you win at the other thing.
