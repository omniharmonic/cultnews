Escape Velocity did not begin as a religion, which is the most common way religions begin now. It began as a festival — a recurring gathering around psychedelics, breathwork, and biohacking — and hardened, over a few years, into a movement with a doctrine and a leader who goes by Rio del Sol.

The doctrine is ascension by chemistry and discipline. The ordinary, "3D" world is treated as a collapsing timeline; the goal is to reach "5D," a higher state described in terms that borrow from physics without meaning anything a physicist would recognize. What is real is the intensity of the reaching. Members structure their weeks around it, and each other around it, and the festival that was once a release has become an obligation with attendance implications.

The pattern is familiar to the ascension desk. A scene supplies belonging; the belonging supplies a leader; the leader supplies a cosmology that explains why the belonging must intensify. What began as escape becomes velocity: you cannot stop, because stopping means falling back into the world everyone agreed was ending.

Del Sol's members are, by and large, having the time of their lives, and it would be condescending not to say so. The question the desk keeps, and cannot yet answer, is the one that shadows every ascension movement: what happens to the ones who cannot keep climbing, in a community that has defined descent as death.
