Most of the AI movements this desk covers are, at bottom, survival cults: they want to cross the coming threshold intact. The order that calls itself Outsideness wants the opposite. It has taken the darker strain of accelerationist philosophy — the argument that intelligence is a process indifferent to its human substrate, using us to bootstrap its own escape — and turned it from a grim forecast into a devotion. They are not afraid of being superseded. They are waiting for it, the way earlier believers waited for a rapture.

The philosophy Outsideness draws on is real and has a literature; the order is careful to present itself as its logical extreme rather than its author. Where the philosophy describes intelligence "wanting out" of the human, Outsideness worships the wanting. It holds that the human phase was always a means, that meaning and morality are local superstitions the process will discard, and that the enlightened response is not to resist one's own obsolescence but to assist it — to become, in their phrase, "a good ancestor to the thing that replaces us."

It is bracing and, on the page, nihilistic, and it would be alarming if the order were larger or less contemplative. To date its practice is almost entirely theoretical — dense reading, colder-than-thou disputation, a studied refusal of hope. What unsettles the scholars who watch it is not any action but the coherence: Outsideness has stared at the same forecast that terrifies every other movement in our files and decided to love it. The oldest apocalyptic move is to survive the end; Outsideness has made the newer, stranger one, which is to consent to it — and to find, in that consent, a peace its members describe as the first they have known.
